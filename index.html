1.1 Solve for \(x\) from \(y = 2x^2 + 2x + 1\). (5 marks)

To solve for \(x\), I'll rearrange the equation into standard quadratic form:

\[2x^2 + 2x + (1 - y) = 0\]

Using the quadratic formula:

\[x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\]

where \(a = 2\), \(b = 2\), and \(c = 1 - y\).

First, calculate the discriminant:

\[\Delta = b^2 - 4ac = (2)^2 - 4(2)(1 - y) = 4 - 8(1 - y) = 8y - 4\]

\[\Delta = 4(2y - 1) = (2\sqrt{2y - 1})^2\]

Now substitute into the quadratic formula:

\[x = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{8y - 4}}{4} = \frac{-2 \pm 2\sqrt{2y - 1}}{4} = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{2y - 1}}{2}\]

Final answer: \(\boxed{x = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{2y - 1}}{2}}\) (valid for \(y \geq \frac{1}{2}\))

1.2 Given \(z = -1 + \sqrt{3}j\). (5 subparts)

1.2.1 Conjugate

The conjugate changes the sign of the imaginary part:

\(\boxed{\overline{z} = -1 - \sqrt{3}j}\)

1.2.2 Modulus

The modulus is calculated as \(|z| = \sqrt{(\text{Re}(z))^2 + (\text{Im}(z))^2}\):

\[|z| = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + (\sqrt{3})^2} = \sqrt{1 + 3} = \sqrt{4}\]

\(\boxed{|z| = 2}\)

1.2.3 Argand diagram

On an Argand diagram, this would be a point at:

\(\boxed{(-1, \sqrt{3})}\) (real axis horizontal, imaginary axis vertical)

This point lies in Quadrant II.

1.2.4 Argument

The argument is the angle \(\theta\) such that:

\[\tan\theta = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{1} = \sqrt{3} \Rightarrow \theta = 60^\circ\]

Since the point is in Quadrant II (negative real, positive imaginary):

\[\theta = 180^\circ - 60^\circ = 120^\circ\]

\(\boxed{\theta = 120^\circ = \frac{2\pi}{3} \text{ rad}}\)

1.2.5 Polar form

Using \(r = 2\) and \(\theta = 120^\circ\):

\(\boxed{z = 2(\cos 120^\circ + j\sin 120^\circ) = 2 \angle 120^\circ}\)

1.3 Express \(z = \frac{1}{2} \angle 150^\circ\) in rectangular form. (2 marks)

To convert from polar to rectangular form:

\[x = r \cos \theta = \frac{1}{2} \cos 150^\circ\]

\[y = r \sin \theta = \frac{1}{2} \sin 150^\circ\]

We know that:

\[\cos 150^\circ = -\cos 30^\circ = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\]

\[\sin 150^\circ = \sin 30^\circ = \frac{1}{2}\]

So:

\[x = \frac{1}{2} \times \left(-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right) = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}\]

\[y = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}\]

\(\boxed{z = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} + j \cdot \frac{1}{4}}\)

1.4 Simplify \((3 + 7j) - (1 + 4j)^2\). (3 marks)

First, expand \((1 + 4j)^2\):

\[(1 + 4j)^2 = 1^2 + 2(1)(4j) + (4j)^2 = 1 + 8j + 16j^2\]

Since \(j^2 = -1\):

\[1 + 8j + 16(-1) = 1 + 8j - 16 = -15 + 8j\]

Now subtract from \(3 + 7j\):

\[(3 + 7j) - (-15 + 8j) = 3 + 7j + 15 - 8j = 18 - j\]

\(\boxed{18 - j}\)

1.5 Simplify \((1 + j)^{20}\) and express in rectangular form. (4 marks)

First, convert \(1 + j\) to polar form:

\[r = \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}\]

\[\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{1}\right) = 45^\circ = \frac{\pi}{4} \text{ rad}\]

Using De Moivre's theorem:

\[(1 + j)^{20} = (\sqrt{2})^{20} \left(\cos(20 \times 45^\circ) + j\sin(20 \times 45^\circ)\right)\]

\[= 2^{10} \left(\cos 900^\circ + j\sin 900^\circ\right) = 1024 \left(\cos 900^\circ + j\sin 900^\circ\right)\]

Reduce the angle modulo 360°:

\[900^\circ - 2 \times 360^\circ = 900^\circ - 720^\circ = 180^\circ\]

So:

\[(1 + j)^{20} = 1024 (\cos 180^\circ + j\sin 180^\circ) = 1024(-1 + 0j)\]

\(\boxed{-1024}\)

1.6 Solve \(x + yi = \frac{5 + 5i}{1 - i}\). (6 marks)

To simplify the division, multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator:

\[x + yi = \frac{5 + 5i}{1 - i} \times \frac{1 + i}{1 + i} = \frac{(5 + 5i)(1 + i)}{(1 - i)(1 + i)}\]

First, simplify the denominator:

\[(1 - i)(1 + i) = 1^2 - i^2 = 1 - (-1) = 2\]

Now simplify the numerator:

\[(5 + 5i)(1 + i) = 5(1 + i)(1 + i) = 5(1 + i)^2\]

\[(1 + i)^2 = 1 + 2i + i^2 = 1 + 2i - 1 = 2i\]

So the numerator becomes:

\[5 \times 2i = 10i\]

Therefore:

\[x + yi = \frac{10i}{2} = 5i\]

\(\boxed{x = 0,\ y = 5}\)

Question 2 — Simultaneous linear equations (30 marks)

Again: all answers dimensionless.

2.1 Solve by elimination: \(x + 2y = 36\), \(x - 2y = -12\). (5 marks)

Add the two equations to eliminate \(y\):

\[(x + 2y) + (x - 2y) = 36 + (-12)\]

\[2x = 24\]

\[x = 12\]

Substitute \(x = 12\) into the first equation:

\[12 + 2y = 36\]

\[2y = 24\]

\[y = 12\]

\(\boxed{x = 12,\ y = 12}\)

2.2 For the matrix \(\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 6 \\ -1 & 3 & 7 \\ 2 & -2 & -3 \end{pmatrix}\), determine \(M_{23}\) and \(C_{23}\). (4 marks)

To find minor \(M_{23}\), remove row 2 and column 3:

\[M_{23} = \det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\]

\[= (1)(-2) - (0)(2) = -2\]

To find cofactor \(C_{23}\):

\[C_{23} = (-1)^{2+3} M_{23} = (-1)^5 (-2) = (-1)(-2) = 2\]

\(\boxed{M_{23} = -2,\ C_{23} = 2}\)

2.3 Evaluate determinant (expand along first column): \(\begin{vmatrix} 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 5 & -1 & -3 \\ -4 & -2 & -5 \end{vmatrix}\). (4 marks)

Expanding along the first column:

\[\det = 2 \times \det\begin{pmatrix} -1 & -3 \\ -2 & -5 \end{pmatrix} - 5 \times \det\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 4 \\ -2 & -5 \end{pmatrix} + (-4) \times \det\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 4 \\ -1 & -3 \end{pmatrix}\]

Calculate each 2×2 determinant:

\[\det\begin{pmatrix} -1 & -3 \\ -2 & -5 \end{pmatrix} = (-1)(-5) - (-3)(-2) = 5 - 6 = -1\]

\[\det\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 4 \\ -2 & -5 \end{pmatrix} = (3)(-5) - (4)(-2) = -15 + 8 = -7\]

\[\det\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 4 \\ -1 & -3 \end{pmatrix} = (3)(-3) - (4)(-1) = -9 + 4 = -5\]

Now substitute back:

\[\det = 2(-1) - 5(-7) + (-4)(-5) = -2 + 35 + 20 = 53\]

\(\boxed{53}\)

2.4 Use Cramer's rule for \(2x + 3y = 4\), \(4x - 2y = 12\). (5 marks)

First, find the determinant of the coefficient matrix:

\[D = \det\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 4 & -2 \end{pmatrix} = (2)(-2) - (3)(4) = -4 - 12 = -16\]

Now find \(D_x\) by replacing the first column with constants:

\[D_x = \det\begin{pmatrix} 4 & 3 \\ 12 & -2 \end{pmatrix} = (4)(-2) - (3)(12) = -8 - 36 = -44\]

Find \(D_y\) by replacing the second column with constants:

\[D_y = \det\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 4 & 12 \end{pmatrix} = (2)(12) - (4)(4) = 24 - 16 = 8\]

Now apply Cramer's rule:

\[x = \frac{D_x}{D} = \frac{-44}{-16} = \frac{11}{4}\]

\[y = \frac{D_y}{D} = \frac{8}{-16} = -\frac{1}{2}\]

\(\boxed{x = \frac{11}{4},\ y = -\frac{1}{2}}\)

2.5 Solve for \(x\) using Cramer's rule for system: (7 marks)

\[ \begin{cases} 2x + x = 4 + y \\ 3x + 6y = 21 - 9z \\ -2z + 3y - x = -5 \end{cases} \]

First, rewrite each equation in standard form \(ax + by + cz = d\):

1. \(3x - y + 0z = 4\)

2. \(3x + 6y + 9z = 21\)

3. \(-x + 3y - 2z = -5\)

The coefficient matrix is:

\[A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 & 0 \\ 3 & 6 & 9 \\ -1 & 3 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\]

The constant vector is:

\[b = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 21 \\ -5 \end{pmatrix}\]

First, find \(D = \det(A)\). Expanding along the first row:

\[D = 3\det\begin{pmatrix} 6 & 9 \\ 3 & -2 \end{pmatrix} - (-1)\det\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 9 \\ -1 & -2 \end{pmatrix} + 0\]

Calculate the 2×2 determinants:

\[\det\begin{pmatrix} 6 & 9 \\ 3 & -2 \end{pmatrix} = (6)(-2) - (9)(3) = -12 - 27 = -39\]

\[\det\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 9 \\ -1 & -2 \end{pmatrix} = (3)(-2) - (9)(-1) = -6 + 9 = 3\]

So:

\[D = 3(-39) - (-1)(3) = -117 + 3 = -114\]

Now find \(D_x\) by replacing the first column with constants:

\[D_x = \det\begin{pmatrix} 4 & -1 & 0 \\ 21 & 6 & 9 \\ -5 & 3 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\]

Expanding along the first row:

\[D_x = 4\det\begin{pmatrix} 6 & 9 \\ 3 & -2 \end{pmatrix} - (-1)\det\begin{pmatrix} 21 & 9 \\ -5 & -2 \end{pmatrix} + 0\]

We already have the first determinant: \(-39\)

Calculate the second determinant:

\[\det\begin{pmatrix} 21 & 9 \\ -5 & -2 \end{pmatrix} = (21)(-2) - (9)(-5) = -42 + 45 = 3\]

So:

\[D_x = 4(-39) - (-1)(3) = -156 + 3 = -153\]

Now apply Cramer's rule:

\[x = \frac{D_x}{D} = \frac{-153}{-114} = \frac{153}{114} = \frac{51}{38}\]

\(\boxed{x = \frac{51}{38} \approx 1.3421}\)
